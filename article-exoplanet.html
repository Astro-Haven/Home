Project Horizon Captures First Confirmed Exoplanet Transit

By: Research Division·10 min read·Exoplanet Science

Project Horizon has achieved its first confirmed exoplanet transit detection — capturing the passage of WASP-17b across its host star with 1.3 millimagnitude photometric precision, coordinated simultaneously across three member telescopes on two continents.

About WASP-17b: A Giant Among Giants

WASP-17b is one of the most unusual exoplanets known to science. Discovered in 2009 by the Wide Angle Search for Planets (WASP) collaboration, it orbits its host star every 3.74 days. What makes WASP-17b extraordinary is that it orbits retrograde — in the opposite direction to its star's rotation.

WASP-17b at a glance: Orbital period 3.74 days · Radius 1.89 R♃ · Mass 0.49 M♃ · Transit depth ~1.5% · Host star magnitude V=11.6

How the Multi-Site Coordination Worked

Three members on two continents — one in Ireland (8-inch SCT), one in Morocco (10-inch Newtonian), and one in Australia (12-inch SCT) — observed simultaneously, with our platform automatically flagging data gaps and switching primary coverage between sites.

What This Achievement Means

The ability to detect exoplanet transits from the ground, with amateur equipment, using distributed coordination is not new — but it remains extraordinary every time it is achieved. The data from this transit has been submitted to the ExoFOP archive.
